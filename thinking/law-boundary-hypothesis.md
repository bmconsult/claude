**Question**: What is law at the constraint boundary?

## The Constraint Set

Legal systems face impossible simultaneous constraints:

1. **Generality**: Rules apply to all similar cases
2. **Particularity**: Justice requires considering specific circumstances
3. **Stability**: Predictability, rule of law
4. **Adaptability**: Responding to new situations
5. **Efficiency**: Timely resolution, reasonable cost
6. **Due process**: Thorough, fair procedures
7. **Finality**: Disputes must end
8. **Correctness**: Getting the right answer

Impossible trade-offs:
- Maximum generality → injustice in edge cases
- Maximum particularity → no predictability
- Maximum stability → can't adapt to change
- Maximum efficiency → may sacrifice due process
- Perfect correctness → never-ending appeals

## Core Hypothesis

**Legal doctrines are stable positions at boundaries of impossible legal optimization.**

Not "discovered truth" but "emerged at constraint intersection."

### 2. Common Law vs. Civil Law (9/10)

These aren't just historical accidents - they're different boundary positions.

**Common Law**: Particularity + Stability through gradual precedent
- High particularity (case-by-case)
- Stability through cumulative decisions
- Adapts slowly via distinguishing cases

**Civil Law**: Generality + Stability through comprehensive codes
- High generality (abstract rules)
- Stability through written law
- Adapts through legislative amendment

**Same constraints, different boundary positions.**

### 3. Constitutional Interpretation Styles (9/10)

Originalism vs. Living Constitution debate is boundary navigation:

**Originalism**: Maximum stability, minimum adaptability
"The meaning is fixed at ratification."

**Living Constitution**: Maximum adaptability, accepts instability
"The meaning evolves with society."

**Textualism, purposivism, etc.**: Different positions on the same frontier.

**The debate is irresolvable** because it's a choice of boundary position, not a factual question.

### 4. Criminal vs. Civil Law (8/10)

Different constraint weighting produces different systems:

**Criminal**:
- Maximum correctness (beyond reasonable doubt)
- State resources for prosecution
- Strong due process protections

**Civil**:
- Balanced correctness (preponderance)
- Private resources for both sides
- Efficiency prioritized more

**Same society, different boundary positions for different stakes.**

### 5. The "Hard Cases" Pattern (10/10)

Why do hard cases exist?

**Because they fall exactly on boundaries.**

Easy cases: Clearly within one constraint's domain
Hard cases: Multiple constraints pull in different directions
Supreme Court cases: Often directly on boundary between competing constraints

**Prediction**: Hard cases cluster at identifiable constraint intersections.

**Implication**: Different judges reach different conclusions not because some are "wrong" but because they prioritize different constraints at the boundary.

### 6. Legal Change as Boundary Shift (9/10)

When underlying constraints shift, legal doctrine must move:

- Technology: Changes efficiency/due process balance (e-discovery, AI in courts)
- Social change: Changes generality/particularity demands (civil rights law)
- Resources: Changes efficiency/correctness trade-offs

**Prediction**: Legal change is predictable from constraint changes.

## The Hart-Fuller Debate Resolved

Hart: Law is rules (positivism)
Fuller: Law has inherent morality (natural law)

**Boundary resolution**: Both are partially right.

- Law IS a system of rules (stable configurations)
- Those configurations MUST satisfy certain constraints to be stable (Fuller's "inner morality" = stability conditions)

**The "inner morality of law" IS the boundary conditions for stable legal systems.**

Fuller's 8 desiderata (generality, promulgation, prospectivity, clarity, non-contradiction, possibility of compliance, constancy, congruence) are CONSTRAINT BOUNDARY MARKERS.
