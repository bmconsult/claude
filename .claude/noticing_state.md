# PHASE 1: RESEARCH (Parallel)

## Agent: Sendak Specialist

### 1. THE CORE INSIGHT
Sendak understood that imagination is how children master unbearable emotions, and he designed the book's physical form to BE that journey—not just describe it.

### 2. SPECIFIC TECHNIQUES

**Visual Pacing as Emotional State**
- Illustrations literally grow as Max's imagination intensifies: starting small (constrained by white space), expanding to fill margins, consuming entire double-page spreads during peak fantasy, then shrinking back as reality returns
- The white space isn't decorative—it's a visual metaphor for emotional containment and release
- "The white space on the first page... serves to cramp Max in—he is contained, even constrained. As the book progresses, the white space around the illustration shrinks, opening up the image"

**Strategic Wordlessness = Reader Agency**
- Three consecutive wordless double-page spreads for the "wild rumpus"—no text to tell children what to feel or how to interpret
- "When artists remove text, they invite readers to decode the pictures for themselves, so every child reads the book in their own unique way"
- Words dropped entirely when emotion peaks—trusting images alone to carry meaning

**Sentence Breaking Creates Anticipation**
- Only 10 sentences in 338 words, but broken MID-THOUGHT across page turns
- "Sendak breaks sentences in the middle, straddling them across the pages throughout the book, not necessarily in conjunction with the punctuation. In 1963, that was pretty revolutionary"
- Creates rhythm and suspense—the page turn itself becomes emotional punctuation

**"Still Hot" = Unconditional Love Without Words**
- Could have ended with reconciliation scene, apology, mother hugging Max
- Instead: just "and it was still hot"—three words on white space proving love through ACTION (she kept it warm), not explanation
- "What makes Sendak's book so compelling is its grounding effect: Max has a tantrum... but he is pulled back by a belief in parental love to a supper 'still hot'"

**Emotional Truth Over Protection**
- "What [children] most yearn for is a bit of truth. Too many parents and too many writers... don't respect the fact that kids know a great deal and suffer a great deal"
- The book is "all variations on the same theme: how children master various feelings – danger, boredom, fear, frustration, jealousy"

### 3. THE THING NOBODY TALKS ABOUT

Everyone says "Sendak was honest about dark emotions." True but surface-level.

The deeper discovery: **He trusted children to be co-authors.**

The wordless spreads don't just show the wild rumpus—they hand interpretive power to the child. No text tells you what Max feels. No narration explains if this is good or bad. The final three words sit alone on white space—YOU decide what "still hot" means.

Sendak said: "You can't protect kids. They know everything." But his craft reveals the corollary: *You also can't control what they make of your story.* The book doesn't have a moral. It has a journey. Children complete it differently.

This is why it endures: it respects children not just as emotional beings, but as *meaning-makers*. Most children's books want to teach. Wild Things wants to collaborate.

### 4. TRANSFERABLE TO THE NOTICING

**Physical Form = Emotional State**
- Don't just illustrate Clara's loneliness—let the page design embody it (white space, small figures, containment)
- As her friendship with the imaginary friend intensifies, let visual elements break boundaries, expand, consume margins
- The return to reality should look/feel different than the opening—showing she's changed

**Strategic Silence = Reader Respect**
- Identify the emotional peak (the equivalent of the "wild rumpus")
- Remove explanatory text—let image/implication do the work
- Trust children to understand what's happening without narration holding their hand

**Rhythm Through Page Architecture**
- Break sentences mid-thought if it creates anticipation
- Use page turns as emotional punctuation
- White space isn't "empty"—it's breathing room for feeling

**Love Shown, Not Told**
- The equivalent of "still hot"—a small, perfect detail that proves love without stating it
- No need for explicit reconciliation or moral lesson
- Children know what unconditional love looks like when they see it in action

**Don't Resolve What They Need to Resolve**
- The Noticing shouldn't end with "and she learned that real friends are better" or "imagination is powerful"
- Leave interpretive space—let children complete the emotional logic themselves
- The book should be a mirror for their experience, not a lesson plan

**The Sendak Standard:**
- 338 words, 10 sentences, 3 wordless spreads
- Every formal choice serves emotional truth
- The book IS the journey it describes

## Agent: Seuss Specialist

### 1. THE CORE INSIGHT
**Rhythm IS the pedagogy.** Seuss discovered that anapestic tetrameter (da-da-DUM da-da-DUM da-da-DUM da-da-DUM) mirrors a galloping heartbeat, creating a neurobiological pattern that strengthens memory formation and phonological awareness. The meter isn't decoration—it's the delivery mechanism for learning.

### 2. SPECIFIC TECHNIQUES (with examples)

**Constraint-Driven Invention**
- *Cat in the Hat*: Written using only 236 words from a list of 348. Frustration with the word list led him to scan for the first two that rhymed: "cat" and "hat."
- *Green Eggs and Ham*: 50-word vocabulary bet. Became the best-selling Seuss book ever (8 million copies).
- **Principle**: Scarcity of resources drives creativity. Constraints force the brain to innovate unconventional solutions.

**The Spacing Effect (Embedded Cognitive Science)**
- *Green Eggs and Ham* introduces new words with several close repetitions (working memory), then gradually spaces occurrences further apart as the story progresses (long-term memory formation).
- This matches optimal learning theory: practice recall just before you're about to forget.
- **Principle**: Repetition isn't random—it follows the brain's natural memory consolidation rhythm.

**Strategic Neologisms (57.6% attached to rhymes)**
- 377 invented words analyzed; books like *On Beyond Zebra* (47), *If I Ran the Zoo* (37)
- Not random gibberish—"carefully crafted elements" that teach language flexibility
- Children infer meaning from context, building independent reading skills
- **Principle**: "I like nonsense. It wakes up the brain cells."

**Subversive Chaos with Resolution**
- Called himself "subversive as hell" but knew "kids can see a moral coming a mile off"
- Cat in the Hat: chaos (Thing One and Thing Two) → restoration of order
- The anarchy is SAFE—it always resolves
- **Principle**: Give children permission to imagine disorder, but provide the psychological safety of restoration.

**Anapestic Tetrameter Mechanics**
- Born from ship's engine rhythm (1936 voyage from Europe)
- Two unstressed syllables + stressed syllable = galloping quality
- "Strong syllable ending allows for awesomely forceful rhymes—great for children learning to read who always want to emphasize the rhyme"
- The whole line "just rolls off the tongue like a song"
- **Principle**: The meter emphasizes end rhymes, making verse more memorable. Decades later, adults still recall entire passages.

### 3. THE THING NOBODY TALKS ABOUT

**The Mother's Pie Chants**

Henrietta Seuss Geisel worked in her father's bakery, memorizing daily pie specials and chanting them to customers. When young Ted couldn't sleep, she would repeat her "pie-selling chants" to him.

**Why this matters**: The anapestic rhythm isn't just literary technique—it's a lullaby, a mnemonic device, a commercial jingle, and a neurological pattern all in one. Seuss learned that RHYTHM SOOTHES AND TEACHES SIMULTANEOUSLY before he learned to read.

The "Seuss sound" is literally the sound of commerce, comfort, and memory formation braided together. It's why his books feel both anarchic (the content) and deeply safe (the rhythm). The chaos happens within a metronome.

### 4. TRANSFERABLE TO THE NOTICING

**For a book about a lonely girl and an imaginary friend:**

- **Rhythm = Relationship**: The friend's voice should have a distinct rhythmic pattern. Not necessarily anapestic tetrameter, but SOME predictable beat that becomes the sonic signature of connection. When Fern is alone, the rhythm breaks or becomes irregular.

- **Constraint Breeds Intimacy**: Limit the vocabulary intentionally. What if the imaginary friend can only use 100 words? Constraint creates memorable, distinctive voice.

- **The Spacing Effect**: If certain phrases repeat between Fern and her friend, space them strategically: close together at first (establishing the relationship), then further apart as the story progresses (deepening into long-term emotional memory).

- **Safe Chaos**: The imaginary friend can be subversive, mischievous, even slightly transgressive—but must always resolve back to safety. Children need permission to imagine disorder within a container of security.

- **Neologisms as Secret Language**: Invented words between Fern and her friend = exclusivity of their bond. The words don't need dictionary definitions; context teaches meaning. This is how real childhood friendships work.

- **Talk as Equals**: Seuss rejected "bunny-bunny books" and "lukewarm treacle." Treat the child reader with the same respect you'd treat an adult. No condescension. Children have "as much right to quality as their elders."

**The Seuss Standard**: If the rhythm can't be read aloud with joy, it fails. If the constraint doesn't force invention, it's pointless. If the chaos doesn't resolve, it's traumatic. If you're preaching, the kids already left.

## Agent: Milne Specialist

### 1. THE CORE INSIGHT
**Milne creates emotional safety through temporal architecture.** He discovered that children (especially those processing trauma) need a returnable space where time circles rather than progresses, where small things become sacred through linguistic elevation, and where all emotional states—melancholy, anxiety, confidence, mania—receive unconditional friendship without judgment or cure.

### 2. SPECIFIC TECHNIQUES (with examples)

**Dual Voice Architecture: FOR vs. TO**
- Milne doesn't just speak TO children or AS a child—he speaks FOR the child's inner experience while maintaining adult narrator presence
- "Christopher Robin's words and narratives are intertwined with A.A. Milne's words and narratives, as Milne is purportedly telling Christopher Robin the stories that Christopher Robin remembers"
- One voice validates ("I understand what you feel"), the other companionably observes ("Let me tell you what happened")
- **Principle**: Children need both validation AND perspective. The dual voice creates intimacy without infantilization—"not a sophisticated adult use of a child's manner of expression, but rather, the expression of a poet who has never lost the ability to think, feel, and express as a child."

**Capital Letters Transform Mundane into Sacred**
- "Very Small Animal," "Bear of Very Little Brain," "Doing Nothing," "Expotition"—ordinary states elevated through capitalization
- Direct characterization becomes ritual through repetition: "Pooh is a Bear of Very Little Brain" repeated like a benediction
- Small size, limited intelligence, anxiety—the very things children fear about themselves—become dignified identities worthy of respect
- **Principle**: "Small Things Were Important." What adults dismiss, children experience as existential. Capitalize it. Make it count.

**Characters as Complete Emotional States (Not Arcs)**
- Eeyore never "gets better"—his melancholy is a permanent, accepted way of being: "Well, I suppose it is...for some" (responding to "Good morning!")
- Piglet remains anxious: "It is hard to be brave when you're a Very Small Animal"—yet performs brave acts anyway
- Tigger stays bouncy, Owl pompous, Rabbit fussy—no one changes, everyone belongs
- Physical traits inform emotional reality: "Donkeys naturally hang their heads a bit low, and they happen to be grey, so it's not a stretch to take the posture and the color and associate them with sadness"
- **Principle**: Friendship isn't fixing each other. It's showing up. "The important thing is, in the end, Pooh and his friends show up for one another in big and small ways through it all."

**The Rhythm of "Doing Nothing"—Temporal Stasis as Comfort**
- "Pooh doesn't rush his speech, he takes lots of breaks as he goes through a sentence"—pauses, meandering, unhurried
- Famous exchange: "What I like doing best is nothing." / "How do you do nothing?" / "Well, it's when people call out at you just as you're going off to do it, 'What are you going to do?' and you say, 'Oh, Nothing,' and then you go and do it."
- Time doesn't progress linearly—it circles, fragments: "temporality lacks a sense of causality; its brief, fragment-like verses, marked by their insistent repetition of 'Whens,' suggest nothing more than a series of past instants that remain apparently unconnected"
- Contrast with modern books: "faster pacing, quicker movements." Pooh offers "a calmer story" where "all you really need is a pleasant set of characters and some charming conversations"
- **Principle**: Safety requires temporal refuge. The Hundred Acre Wood is "safety to which return is always desirable"—not because it's perfect, but because time there doesn't wound. You can always go back.

**Post-War Gentleness: Trauma Transformed into Returnable Space**
- Milne and illustrator E.H. Shepard both "fought and survived the First World War, 'that nightmare of mental and moral degradation.' They suffered just as much as you'd expect. Shell shock, trauma…but on return both chose to focus on the Hundred Acre Wood"
- The bear himself "was said to be very shy and solitary, similar to how Milne felt after the war"
- "Drawing Winnipeg into the story, reincarnated as Winnie-the-Pooh, was Milne's way of placing himself next to his son, Christopher, throughout their journeys"
- "Writing became Milne's refuge, a way to process his experiences and offer solace to others"
- **Principle**: Don't hide the wound—transform it into shelter. "The film stealthily posits that the time in our childhoods that we spend imagining and pretending, those are the moments that make us good people. Not school, not work, not duty to our countries, but play."

### 3. THE THING NOBODY TALKS ABOUT

Everyone says "Milne was gentle and whimsical." True but incomplete.

The deeper discovery: **The Hundred Acre Wood is PTSD architecture.**

Milne didn't write comfort stories despite his war trauma—he wrote them BECAUSE of it. Every formal choice is therapeutic design:
- **Time that doesn't progress** = escape from linear trauma processing
- **Unconditional acceptance of all emotional states** = the opposite of battlefield judgment (brave/coward)
- **"Doing Nothing" elevated to philosophy** = antidote to war's demand for constant action/vigilance
- **Small things made sacred** = reclaiming gentleness after mechanized violence
- **Characters never change** = you don't have to "get better" to deserve love

"Winnie-the-Pooh were written as a father's way to explain his post-traumatic stress to his child." But it's more: it's **trauma reverse-engineered into a space where healing is optional and presence is enough.**

This is why it endures cross-culturally: war trauma exists everywhere. The need for a returnable safety transcends culture. Milne didn't write FOR children—he wrote a space where the traumatized parts of ALL of us (child and adult) can rest.

### 4. TRANSFERABLE TO THE NOTICING

**For a book about a lonely girl and an imaginary friend:**

- **The Friend as Returnable Safety**: The imaginary friend isn't just company—it's architecture. A space Fern can return to when the world demands too much. Time with the friend should feel different: slower, circling, fragment-like. The friend doesn't push Fern toward "growth"—just presence.

- **Elevate Her Smallness**: If Fern feels insignificant, invisible, "very small"—CAPITALIZE IT. "A Very Quiet Girl." "The One Who Notices." Make her perceived weakness her dignified identity. Small doesn't need to become Big. Small deserves reverence as-is.

- **All Emotional States Belong**: If Fern is lonely, sad, anxious—don't arc toward "and then she learned to be brave." Let her be a Lonely Girl who sometimes does brave things anyway, like Piglet. The friend accepts all of her, permanently, without fix-it energy.

- **The Rhythm of Friendship = Meandering Presence**: Real friendship isn't plot. It's "going along, listening to all the things you can't hear, and not bothering." Let scenes meander. Let dialogue pause mid-thought. Let the friend and Fern do Nothing together, and make that Nothing sacred.

- **Dual Voice for Validation**: Fern's internal voice (child experiencing) + narrative voice (gentle witness) = the Milne architecture. One validates ("I know this feels big"), the other accompanies ("Let me be here with you"). Never condescend.

- **Build a Geography of Return**: Like the Hundred Acre Wood, create a PLACE (real or imagined) that Fern can always return to. Not because problems are solved there, but because time there doesn't demand anything. It's "safety to which return is always desirable."

**The Milne Standard**:
- Does time feel different in the friendship? (slower, gentler, circular)
- Are small things treated as genuinely important, not cutely so?
- Can the character stay broken and still be loved?
- Would this comfort someone carrying a wound they can't name?
- If you removed the friend, would the architecture of safety collapse—or has it taught Fern to build her own?

# PHASE 2: SYNTHESIS

## The Unified Standard

### The Three Agree On:

1. **Children are complete beings, not beings-in-progress**
   - Sendak: "Kids know everything" — they experience full emotional complexity now, not later
   - Seuss: Children have "as much right to quality as their elders" — no dumbing down
   - Milne: Characters never "grow up" or "get better" — they deserve love as-is

2. **Form serves emotion, not decoration**
   - Sendak: Page architecture (white space, image size) = containment/release
   - Seuss: Anapestic tetrameter = neurological delivery mechanism for memory
   - Milne: Temporal circularity = therapeutic refuge from linear trauma

3. **Strategic withholding creates power**
   - Sendak: Wordless spreads = reader agency (you complete the meaning)
   - Seuss: Vocabulary constraint = forced invention (50 words → bestseller)
   - Milne: "Doing Nothing" = sacred space (absence elevated to philosophy)

4. **Don't preach, don't fix**
   - Sendak: No moral lesson — "still hot" proves love without explaining it
   - Seuss: "Kids can see a moral coming a mile off" — subversive as hell
   - Milne: Eeyore stays depressed, Piglet stays anxious — friendship isn't cure

5. **The book IS the experience, not about it**
   - Sendak: The expanding images ARE imagination intensifying (not pictures of it)
   - Seuss: The rhythm IS memory formation (not description of learning)
   - Milne: The circular time IS refuge (not a story about finding refuge)

### The Three Diverge On:

1. **Temporal structure**
   - Sendak: Linear journey (there and back) — imagination as round trip
   - Seuss: Forward momentum with resolution — chaos always resolves to order
   - Milne: Circular, fragment-like, no causality — "safety to which return is always desirable"
   - **For THE NOTICING**: Milne's circularity wins. Loneliness isn't cured by friendship—it's a state you return to and leave, cyclically. The friend offers *refuge*, not *resolution*.

2. **Energy signature**
   - Sendak: Wild → calm (emotional arc from rage to reconciliation)
   - Seuss: Manic throughout, but contained (galloping rhythm never slows)
   - Milne: Gentle, meandering, unhurried ("Pooh doesn't rush his speech")
   - **For THE NOTICING**: Hybrid approach. When the friend is present: Milne's meander. When Clara is alone: Sendak's visual tension (cramped, constrained). The shift in pacing IS the relationship.

3. **Voice architecture**
   - Sendak: Tight third-person (338 words, 10 sentences — every syllable earns its place)
   - Seuss: Rhythmic constraint (anapestic tetrameter as iron law)
   - Milne: Dual voice (child experiencing + narrator accompanying)
   - **For THE NOTICING**: Milne's dual voice, but with Sendak's economy. Validation (FOR Clara) + witness (WITH Clara), zero fat.

4. **The wound they address**
   - Sendak: Rage and powerlessness ("I'll eat you up!")
   - Seuss: Illiteracy and boredom (make reading joyful, not dutiful)
   - Milne: War trauma and existential unsafety (build returnable shelter)
   - **For THE NOTICING**: Clara's loneliness is closer to Milne's wound (lack of safety/belonging) than Sendak's (lack of power). The friend is architecture, not catharsis.

### The Golden Thread:

**The book must be a TECHNOLOGY for the child's inner life, not ABOUT it.**

All three discovered the same truth through different doors:

- Don't *represent* the child's experience
- *CREATE* the experience

The form doesn't illustrate the theme. The form IS the theme made flesh.

- Sendak: Reading Wild Things doesn't describe imagination—it ACTIVATES yours (wordless spreads hand you interpretive power)
- Seuss: Reading Green Eggs and Ham doesn't describe learning—it IS learning (your brain forms phonological patterns while you read)
- Milne: Reading Pooh doesn't describe safety—it BECOMES safety (you return to the Hundred Acre Wood when you need refuge)

**The test**: Does the book DO something to you, or just tell you about something?

### The 7 Tests for THE NOTICING:

1. **The Form Test** (Sendak)
   Does the visual/textual architecture *embody* Clara's emotional state, or just illustrate it?
   - Can you SEE loneliness in white space, smallness, containment?
   - Does the friend's presence break visual boundaries (margins, page spreads)?
   - Does the final page look/feel different than the first (showing she's changed without saying it)?

2. **The Co-Author Test** (Sendak)
   Are there moments where interpretation is deliberately left open?
   - Does the book trust the child to complete meaning without narration?
   - Is there a wordless sequence where the friend's "realness" is ambiguous?
   - Does the ending resist easy resolution ("still hot" = love proven by action, not words)?

3. **The Rhythm Test** (Seuss)
   Does the friend have a distinct sonic signature?
   - When present: does language acquire a predictable rhythm/pattern (security)?
   - When absent: does rhythm break or become irregular (insecurity)?
   - Is there a "secret language" between Clara and the friend (neologisms, repeated phrases)?

4. **The Constraint Test** (Seuss)
   Has some formal limitation forced unexpected invention?
   - Limited vocabulary (what if the friend can only use 100 words)?
   - Visual constraint (single color for friend, greyscale for reality)?
   - Sentence structure (what if all friend-dialogue is questions, or fragments)?

5. **The Acceptance Test** (Milne)
   Does the friend accept ALL of Clara's emotional states without trying to fix her?
   - Can she stay lonely, sad, small — and still be loved?
   - Does the friend ever say "don't feel that way" or push toward growth?
   - Are her "flaws" (quietness, invisibility) elevated to sacred identity ("The Very Quiet One")?

6. **The Returnable Safety Test** (Milne)
   Is there a space (real/imagined) Clara can return to?
   - Does time feel *different* there (slower, gentler, circular vs. linear)?
   - When she's with the friend, does the prose meander, pause mid-thought, linger?
   - Can she do "Nothing" with the friend, and is that Nothing dignified?

7. **The Technology Test** (ALL THREE)
   Does reading the book CREATE an experience, or just describe one?
   - After reading, can a lonely child return to the book as Clara returns to the friend?
   - Does the book become a tool for their inner life (not just a story they consumed)?
   - Will it DO something to them (activate imagination, provide refuge, form memory) — or just tell them something?

**Ruthless standard**: If the answer to any test is "no" or "sort of," the book isn't there yet.

These aren't aspirational. They're the entry fee for classic status.

This could be a wordless sequence. The crescendo of "SEE ME" could be VISUAL (italics enlarging, fragmenting across the page, consuming space). Instead you narrate it: "Too many hands. Too many mouths." You describe the overwhelm rather than creating it.

**SPECIFIC FIX:**

- **Wisp's nature**: Never explicitly resolve whether Wisp is real, imaginary, or something else. Sendak left the Wild Things ambiguous—dream? imagination? real island? He never tells you. You shouldn't either.

Stop explaining. Start trusting.

**WHY IT FAILS:**
No sonic signature. Wisp sounds like narrative prose broken into dialogue tags. Compare to Seuss: "The rhythm IS the relationship." When Pooh speaks in Milne, you hear "Pooh doesn't rush his speech, he takes lots of breaks."

Wisp has no musical identity. I can't "hear" this character.

**QUOTE (Mote alone vs. with Wisp):**
Compare rhythm when alone: "She talked to them when no one else was listening. Which was often."

With Wisp: "Wisp took Mote sideways—through a direction she hadn't known existed."

No difference. Same staccato prose. Same short declarative sentences.

**WHY IT FAILS:**
The prose should CHANGE when Wisp appears. Rhythm = relationship. If friendship alters her inner state, it should alter the language's music.

**SPECIFIC FIX:**
- **Wisp's speech pattern**: Every line Wisp speaks should have a rhythmic pattern. Not anapestic tetrameter necessarily, but SOME beat. Maybe Wisp speaks in questions? In fragments? In rhyme? In repetition-with-variation?
  - Option: "See me? / Really see me? / See me as I was / and am / and could be?"
- **When Wisp is present**: Sentences lengthen, meander, breathe. Commas multiply. Thoughts trail off mid...
- **When Mote is alone**: Sentences shorten. Fragment. Stop.
- **Secret language**: Create 3-5 words that only Mote and Wisp use. Neologisms. "Sideways" is close—but needs to be THEIR word, not just description. "We'll go shimmer-ways." "The glow-between." Make language their bond.

Right now the prose is rhythmically flat. Give Wisp a voice I can hear with my eyes closed.

### 4. Constraint Test: 2/10

**WHY IT FAILS:**
I see zero evidence of formal constraint. Vocabulary is unrestricted. Sentence structure varies freely. You're writing "normal" prose.

Compare:
- Sendak: 338 words, 10 sentences for entire book
- Seuss: 50-word vocabulary bet
- This draft: no constraint = no forced invention

**SPECIFIC FIX:**
Choose ONE constraint and make it iron law:

**OPTION A: Vocabulary Limit**
- Wisp can only use 100 words (like Green Eggs and Ham's 50)
- Forces you to create meaning through repetition, combination, rhythm
- Makes Wisp's voice DISTINCT from Mote's, from narrator's
- Tests: Can you tell the entire friendship arc with 100 words?

**OPTION B: Sendak-style Economy**
- Cut to 400 words TOTAL for the whole book
- Every word earns its place
- Forces visual storytelling to carry weight
- Tests: What can you cut without losing emotional truth?

**OPTION C: Structural Constraint**
- All of Mote's dialogue is statements. All of Wisp's is questions. (Or inverse.)
- Creates call-and-response rhythm
- Makes their voices architecturally distinct
- Tests: Can questions/statements alone carry the relationship?

**OPTION D: Visual Constraint**

### 7. Technology Test: 3/10

The book is a well-crafted STORY. It's not yet a TECHNOLOGY.
